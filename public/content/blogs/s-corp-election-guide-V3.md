## What Is S-Corp Election? Understanding S-Corporation Tax Status

First, let's clear up confusion:

**An S-Corp is not a business entity.** It's a **tax election**. You're still an LLC or corporation. You're just choosing to be taxed differently.

**Without S-Corp election:**
- Single-member LLC = Taxed as sole proprietor
- Multi-member LLC = Taxed as partnership
- Corporation = Taxed as C-Corp (double taxation)

**With S-Corp election:**
- Your LLC or corporation is taxed as an S-Corporation
- You become an employee of your own business
- You split your income between salary and distributions
- **Distributions are not subject to self-employment tax**

That last bullet is the entire reason S-Corp exists. It saves self-employment (SE) tax. That's it.

## What S-Corp Actually Solves (And What It Doesn't)

**S-Corp solves ONE problem: self-employment tax on distributions.**

That's it. It doesn't:
- ‚ùå Reduce your income tax (same tax bracket, same deductions)
- ‚ùå Protect assets better than an LLC (same liability protection)
- ‚ùå Make accounting simpler (opposite‚Äîit's MORE complex)
- ‚ùå Solve cash flow problems (you still owe taxes on profit)
- ‚ùå Help you raise capital (actually makes it harder)
- ‚ùå Work for passive income (rental income, investments aren't subject to SE tax anyway)

**It's a specialized tool for one specific tax situation.** Don't elect S-Corp because "successful businesses are S-Corps." Elect because the math works for your specific situation.

## When Does S-Corp Election Make Sense?

### ‚úÖ You're a Good Candidate If:

**1. Your net profit is consistently over $100K/year**  
Below $100K, the administrative costs ($7,000-8,000/year) often outweigh the tax savings. Between $100K-$120K, it's breakeven territory‚Äîworth considering if you expect growth. Above $120K, it starts making clear financial sense.

**2. You're in a service-based business with simple structure**  
Consultants, **healthcare providers**, attorneys, accountants, designers, coaches‚Äîanyone selling expertise from a single operating entity.

**3. You're already profitable and expect to stay that way**  
S-Corp doesn't make sense for startups burning cash or businesses with highly variable income.

**4. You're willing to run payroll consistently**  
You'll need to pay yourself via W-2 every month, which means [payroll setup](/services), quarterly 941s, and year-end W-2s. No skipping months.

**5. You understand you're only saving SE tax**  
If you think S-Corp will cut your total tax bill in half, pump the brakes. Income tax stays the same. You're only saving 15.3% on distributions.

## When S-Corp Is NOT the Answer

You probably should NOT elect S-Corp if:

### ‚ùå 1. Your Net Profit Is Under $100K Consistently

The math doesn't work. Let's prove it:

**At $80K net profit:**
- Reasonable salary: $55K
- Distributions: $25K
- SE tax savings on $25K: $3,825
- S-Corp costs: $7,000
- **NET RESULT: LOSING $3,175/year**

You'd be paying $3,175 extra just for the privilege of having an S-Corp.

### ‚ùå 2. You Have Highly Variable Income

Bad year with $60K profit? You still owe payroll taxes on your salary, even if the business barely broke even. S-Corp works best with *predictable* profit.

**Example:** You set salary at $70K expecting $150K profit. Actual profit = $80K. You're paying payroll taxes on $70K salary from only $80K total profit. That hurts.

### ‚ùå 3. You Operate Multiple Entities (See Next Section)

Unless you have one clearly dominant entity generating $200K+, the complexity may not be worth it. Multi-entity S-Corp structures are a completely different animal.

### ‚ùå 4. You Don't Understand WHY You're Doing It

If your only reasoning is "my buddy said I should," stop. You need to understand:
- How reasonable salary works
- Why distributions aren't subject to SE tax (but ARE subject to income tax)
- What compliance looks like
- The specific dollar amount you'll save after costs

### ‚ùå 5. You're Planning to Sell the Business Soon

S-Corp can complicate business sales. Built-in gains, basis adjustments, and buyer preferences matter. Talk to your M&A advisor first.

### ‚ùå 6. You Want to Retain Earnings Long-Term

S-Corps are pass-through‚Äîyou're taxed on profit whether you take it out or not. If you're reinvesting heavily and not taking distributions, you'll pay tax on phantom income.

### ‚ùå 7. Your Business Is Real Estate Rental Income

Rental income isn't subject to SE tax anyway. S-Corp adds complexity with zero benefit.

### ‚ùå 8. You're a 1099 Contractor Maxing Retirement Contributions

Solo 401(k) contribution limits can be better as a sole prop in some cases. Run the numbers first with your EA.

## Multi-Entity Structures: When S-Corp Gets Complicated

**S-Corp is NOT an automatic choice when you have multiple operating entities.**

If you operate several LLCs (common in real estate, multi-location practices, or holding company structures), S-Corp election gets complex fast‚Äîand may not be worth it.

### The Complications:

**1. Which entity elects S-Corp status?**
- Operating company? Holding company? All of them?
- Each entity = separate S-Corp election + separate payroll + separate Form 1120-S
- **Example:** 3 entities = 3 payrolls, 3 S-Corp returns, 3 reasonable compensation analyses

**2. Reasonable salary allocation gets messy**
- If you work across 3 entities, how do you split your salary?
- IRS wants to see salary from each entity where you provide substantial services
- Multiple W-2s from your own entities raises audit flags
- **Example:** You work 40% in Entity A, 40% in Entity B, 20% in Entity C. Do you split salary 40/40/20? What if Entity C lost money?

**3. Administrative burden multiplies**
- 1 S-Corp: $7,000/year in costs
- 3 S-Corps: $15,000-20,000/year in costs
- **At what point does the complexity outweigh the savings?**

**4. Inter-company transactions require documentation**
- Management fees between entities need written agreements
- Related-party transactions are IRS audit triggers
- Sloppy inter-company accounting = major problems
- **Example:** Entity A "pays" Entity B for services. Without proper documentation, IRS may disallow the deduction.

**5. Cost allocation nightmares**
- Shared expenses (rent, utilities, admin staff) must be allocated across entities
- Requires detailed timesheets, usage logs, and allocation methods
- One mistake = entire structure at risk in audit

### When Multi-Entity S-Corp DOES Make Sense:

‚úÖ **Single high-profit entity** ($200K+) with passive holding companies  
‚úÖ **Clear operational separation** (different services, different clients, no shared expenses)  
‚úÖ **You're willing to invest in proper structure** (legal agreements, clean books, detailed allocation)  
‚úÖ **You have professional accounting support** managing the complexity

### When to Keep It Simple:

‚ùå Multiple small entities (each under $150K profit)  
‚ùå Entities with significant related-party transactions  
‚ùå Entities with shared expenses that are hard to allocate  
‚ùå You're not ready for the compliance overhead  
‚ùå Your current accountant isn't experienced with multi-entity S-Corp structures

**Bottom line for multi-entity structures:** This needs *custom analysis*, not a one-size-fits-all S-Corp election.

## The "Reasonable Salary" Question

Here's where business owners get nervous:

**The IRS requires you to pay yourself a "reasonable salary" before taking distributions.**

Too low of a salary = audit risk.  
Too high = you're not maximizing savings.

So what's "reasonable"?

**The IRS looks at:**
- What similar positions earn in your industry
- Your role and responsibilities in the business
- Time spent working
- Business profitability

**General guidelines for single-entity businesses:**
- **$60K-$80K** for most service-based businesses in the $120K-$150K profit range
- **$80K-$120K** if you're the primary revenue generator and profit is $200K+
- **$50K-$70K** if you have other team members doing the work and you're more of an owner/manager

**Quick test:** Could you hire someone to do your job for this salary? If yes, it's probably reasonable.

### Missed the March 15th Deadline? You're Not Out of Options

**The IRS offers late S-Corp election relief** if you miss the deadline.

**How it works:**
- File Form 2553 with a "reasonable cause" statement attached
- Explain why you missed the deadline (e.g., relied on tax professional who missed it, didn't know about deadline, etc.)
- IRS grants relief automatically if you meet certain conditions

**Conditions for automatic relief:**
1. You file within **3 years and 75 days** of the desired effective date
2. You and all shareholders report income consistent with S-Corp status
3. You have reasonable cause for missing the deadline

**Example:** You want S-Corp status for 2026 but missed March 15, 2026. You can still file late in 2026, 2027, or even early 2029 and request retroactive S-Corp treatment back to January 1, 2026.

**The catch:** 
- You must have been *operating* as an S-Corp (paying yourself salary, filing as if you were an S-Corp) even if you didn't officially elect
- Can't just decide retroactively "I wish I had been an S-Corp"
- Requires showing you intended to be an S-Corp and just missed the paperwork deadline

**Practical advice:**
- If you miss March 15th, don't panic
- File Form 2553 as soon as you realize the mistake
- Include a reasonable cause statement
- Start operating as an S-Corp immediately (set up payroll, pay yourself salary)
- Work with an EA who has experience with late elections

## FAQ: S-Corp Election

### What is S-Corp election?

S-Corp election (Form 2553) allows your LLC or corporation to be taxed as an S-Corporation. You split income between salary (subject to payroll taxes) and distributions (not subject to SE tax). Only saves SE tax‚Äîincome tax stays the same.

### When should I elect S-Corp status?

Elect S-Corp when your net profit consistently exceeds $100K per year AND you pass the readiness checklist. File Form 2553 by March 15th for current-year election. Below $100K, costs usually outweigh savings.

### How much does S-Corp election actually cost?

Realistic annual costs are $7,000-$8,000 (payroll, S-Corp return, bookkeeping, EA support). Setup is $500-$1,500. Lower advertised costs don't include professional guidance‚Äîwhich you'll want when the IRS comes knocking.

### What is a reasonable salary for S-Corp?

Reasonable salary matches what you'd pay someone to do your job. Typical ranges: $60K-$80K for $120K-$150K profit, $80K-$120K for $200K+ profit. Must be documented and defensible in an audit.

### Can I elect S-Corp with multiple LLCs?

Yes, but it's complicated. Each entity needs separate S-Corp election, payroll, and reasonable salary analysis. Costs multiply. Often NOT worth it unless one entity has $200K+ profit. Requires custom analysis.

### What's the deadline to elect S-Corp for 2026?

March 15, 2026 for calendar-year businesses. File in January-February to avoid processing delays. **Missed it? Late election relief is available**‚Äîyou can file retroactively within 3 years and 75 days with reasonable cause. See "Missed the Deadline?" section above.

### Can I file S-Corp election after the March 15th deadline?

Yes! The IRS offers late election relief if you have reasonable cause. File Form 2553 with a statement explaining why you missed the deadline. You can file up to 3 years and 75 days after the desired effective date. Requires showing you operated as an S-Corp (paid salary, reported as S-Corp) even though paperwork was late. Work with an EA experienced in late elections‚Äîit's fixable.

### Does S-Corp make sense if I have variable income?

Usually no. If you set salary at $80K expecting $150K profit, but only make $90K, you're stuck paying payroll taxes on $80K salary from $90K profit. S-Corp works best with predictable profit.
