Client Summary

STATUS: NEEDS ATTENTION

Center A generated $5.07M in revenue over the trailing twelve months with an 18.5% net margin and $576K in cash — solid numbers on the surface. The practice is profitable, the debt service coverage ratio is healthy at 2.71x, and the balance sheet carries no accounts receivable because billing flows through the parent entity.

However, three issues require immediate attention:

  • Collection efficiency is deteriorating. The dollars collected per claim dropped 15% year-over-year at Center A, and the broader multi-center group shows a 10% decline over the last six months. This is not a volume problem — it is a reimbursement and execution problem, concentrated in Managed Care claims.
  • Owner distributions are consuming 64% of EBITDA. Combined with debt service at 37%, total cash outflows exceed 100% of EBITDA. The entity is generating zero free cash flow and slowly drawing down its cash reserves.
  • Revenue is softening. February 2026 revenue of $386K is 12.5% below February 2025 ($441K), and January hit a TTM low of $343K. The 6-month moving average is trending down.

None of these are emergencies today. Cash reserves provide 54 days of runway, and the DSCR is well above banking covenants. But the trajectory needs correcting before Q3 2026. Your team at Benefique is on this.

Payroll Deep Dive: +7.3 Points Decomposition

The payroll-to-revenue ratio jumped from 17.8% to 25.1%. This looks alarming, but the driver is a mix of two forces:

1. Absolute $ increase: Feb 25 payroll = $78,506 (17.8% of $440,824). Feb 26 payroll = $96,714. That is an $18,208 increase in actual payroll dollars — a 23% raise in headcount or wage cost.

2. Denominator effect: Revenue dropped from $441K to $386K (-12.5%). If payroll had stayed at $78.5K, it would still show 20.4% of Feb 26 revenue (a +2.6pt increase) just from the smaller revenue base. The remaining +4.7pts is real cost growth.

Conservative target: Bringing payroll to 21% of revenue = $80,986/mo at Feb revenue. Savings: $15,728/mo = $189K/year.

5. Cash Management (continued)

Three-Lens Narrative

Operational
Distributions consumed 64% of EBITDA ($686K of $1,075K). This is the single largest cash outflow and the primary reason the entity cannot build reserves. At current revenue levels, there is no margin for error — one bad collection month tips cash flow negative.
Banking
Combined outflows (distributions + debt service) equal 101% of EBITDA. A banker sees a business that generates just enough to pay its obligations but retains nothing. This is a covenant risk if revenue declines another 5-10%.
Valuation
Zero free cash flow compresses the purchase multiple. A buyer paying 4-5x EBITDA expects retained earnings to justify the price. When 101% flows out, the buyer discounts aggressively — or structures an earnout tied to retention improvements.

What a Banker Sees That You Don't

A commercial lender reviewing this entity sees three things that the owner might not focus on:

1. Equity erosion from distributions. Three months ago, equity was $405K. Today it is $150K. The entity earned money but distributed more than it retained. Bankers track this quarter-over-quarter and view it as ownership extracting value faster than the business creates it.

2. The 101% payout ratio. Distributions ($686K) + debt service ($397K) = $1,084K, which is 101% of EBITDA ($1,075K). In a banker's model, this means the entity cannot self-fund growth, absorb a downturn, or build reserves without external capital.

3. Declining revenue with stable debt. Revenue is trending down 4.2% while debt remains at $1.46M. The leverage ratio (1.36x) is fine today, but if revenue drops another 15%, leverage crosses 2.0x and the DSCR approaches covenant territory.
